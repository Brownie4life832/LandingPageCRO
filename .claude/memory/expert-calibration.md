## Expert Priorities

The expert's analysis follows a clear hierarchy — these are ranked by how early and emphatically they come up:

### 1. HEADLINE ABOVE THE FOLD (non-negotiable, always first)
- **This is the #1 thing the expert looks at on any page.** Exact quote pattern: "first thing I look at when I look at any website is what am I immediately seeing above the fold"
- The headline MUST be visible without scrolling on mobile. If it's not, nothing else matters.
- On the stepper page, the headline was cut off on his Galaxy S25 Plus — an immediate fail.
- Expert thinks about phone sizes: "people have a lot smaller phones than I do" — so design for the smallest common screen, not the biggest.
- **Rule: If the headline isn't above the fold on mobile, that's the #1 fix. Everything else is secondary.**

### 2. MOBILE-FIRST ALWAYS (90% of traffic)
- "90% of site visits" come from mobile — analysis is done exclusively on mobile view.
- All sizing, spacing, and readability judgments are made through a mobile lens.
- If text is too small to read on a phone, it's a dealbreaker. "I can't imagine my mom reading this because it's just too small."
- The expert literally tests on his own phone (Galaxy S25 Plus/Ultra) to validate.

### 3. VALUE PROPS MUST BE VISIBLE AND BENEFIT-DRIVEN (not buried in tiny text)
- Value props should hit you in the face while scrolling. If you have to stop and read small text to find them, they're wasted.
- "You have 13 seconds to capture my attention and make me read, you need to hammer me with what I'm gonna feel when I'm looking at this."
- Expert scrolls quickly to simulate real user behavior — if value props aren't caught during a fast scroll, they fail.
- Features (LCD display, anti-slip pedals, magnetic resistance) are "meaningless jargon" — the expert wants benefits that solve a job.
- **Rule: Don't say "there's benefits" — just state the benefit. "Boost circulation and reduce the swelling. Boom."**

### 4. CTA CONSISTENCY AND SIZE
- All CTAs on the page should use the same copy and color. "Just keep the order yours today across all of them"
- Different CTAs ("Step into fitness," "Upgrade your routine," "Order yours today") is "weird" and confusing.
- CTAs are "all way too small" — they need to be full-screen-width on mobile.
- Same color across all CTAs so it "connects a bell on the page that they should click this."

### 5. REMOVE DISTRACTIONS FROM LANDING PAGES
- Sitewide promotional banners (jewelry sale) should be removed for landing page traffic arriving from ads.
- "If they come from the ad they don't get this banner that'd be amazing it's not relevant it takes up so much room"
- Discount codes that are already being advertised don't need a banner — "we don't need to advertise this massive discount code up here per se when we have this code that they can use that's already being advertised"
- **Rule: If the page receives traffic from a specific UTM/ad, strip everything that isn't about the product.**

### 6. PAGE MUST EXPLAIN THE PRODUCT WITHOUT THE VIDEO (new from Review 2)
- Expert scrolled the entire cane page and said: "I don't know what this does. I'm gonna be honest with you. You've told me it's smart but I don't know what smart means."
- Only AFTER watching the embedded video did the expert understand the product: "I just watched the video and this product is so cool... except I got none of that when I scroll through this page."
- The static page content must communicate everything the video shows. You cannot rely on the visitor watching the video.
- **Rule: If a visitor can scroll the full page and still not understand what the product does or why it's different, the page has failed its primary job.**
- If you call a product "smart," you must immediately explain what makes it smart. Vague descriptors without explanation are worse than no descriptor.

### 7. GREAT COPY MUST BE PLACED HIGH, NOT BURIED (new from Review 2)
- "Step with Joy. Walk with Confidence" — expert called this "fantastic" and was frustrated it was buried near the bottom: "There is no reason that this is not up here."
- When you have emotionally resonant, benefit-driven copy, it needs to be the #2 thing on the page (right after the headline), not the 8th section.
- **Rule: If your best copy is below the fold, you've wasted your best ammo.**

### 8. IMAGES MUST MATCH THE COPY
- "Stay active from the comfort of your chair" but the main image doesn't show someone sitting in a chair — that's a disconnect.
- "They immediately want to see themselves relaxing in the chair working out and not having this right here, not having the person sitting in the chair in the front can really affect that."
- Product-in-use imagery should match the headline's promise exactly.

## Scoring Tendencies

### Strict On:
- **Above-the-fold headline visibility**: Binary pass/fail. If it's not visible on mobile, the page is broken.
- **CTA size on mobile**: Must be large, full-width, impossible to miss. Small CTAs are always flagged.
- **Value prop visibility during fast scroll**: If you can't catch the value props while scrolling at normal speed, they don't count.
- **Text readability for older audiences**: "I can't imagine my mom reading this" — if it's not legible for a 60+ year old, it fails.
- **Product comprehension without video**: If the expert can scroll the whole page and not understand what the product does, that's a fundamental fail. (Confirmed in Review 2)
- **Meaningless aspirational phrases**: "Power your walk with smart support and all the comfort" = "relatively meaningless phrases." If a phrase could apply to any product, it communicates nothing. (Confirmed in Review 2)
- **White space waste**: Large white space on a landing page (especially PDP sections) that doesn't contain value props is "distracting" and "wasted." Fill it with benefits or remove it. (Review 2)

### Lenient On:
- **Star ratings / "Best in Comfort Fitness" labels**: "This doesn't mean anything right... this should not get in the way of the headline." — Not a dealbreaker if present, but shouldn't take prime real estate.
- **Testimonial text size**: "Testimonial text can be small, that's okay" — testimonials don't need to be huge, just present.
- **Feature highlight sections**: Acknowledges they're "nice" but secondary to benefit-driven messaging.
- **Testimonials with product-in-use photos**: "I like these testimonials, this is really cool. And it's cool to see if you watch your using the product." — Testimonials are valued, not scrutinized harshly. (Review 2)

## Recurring Themes

### Theme 1: Speed of comprehension
- Users come from TikTok, Meta, Instagram — they're in scroll mode.
- "You got 20 seconds to convince them... you usually get like eight to ten"
- Everything must communicate value during a fast scroll — no stopping to read required.

### Theme 2: Benefits over features, always
- LCD Display, Anti-Slip Pedals, Magnetic Resistance = "meaningless jargon that doesn't really do anything for me"
- "12 modes to fit every level of workout" = much better because it implies personalization.
- Remote control = worth highlighting because "the remote control plays a big factor into your ability to use it from the chair"

### Theme 3: The "mom test"
- Expert benchmarks readability against whether his mom (older demographic) could read and understand the page on a phone.
- If copy is too small or too clever, it fails the mom test.

### Theme 4: Reduce, don't add
- Expert favors reducing sections rather than adding more. "Maybe we reduce two of these and focus"
- Small, non-powerful sections should be removed or merged: "these are small to the point where I'm not sure they really add much value"
- If highlighting a feature, give it a specific headline or make it much bigger — don't leave it as a tiny card.

### Theme 5: Zero fall risk = massive selling point
- "Zero fall risk, that's a big value proposition for people who are using this" — expert specifically called this out as underutilized.
- Should be a headline or prominent callout, not buried in body copy.
- Review 2 reinforced: "reduce your fall risk" listed as a top headline direction for the smart cane.

### Theme 6: Family confidence / peace of mind (new from Review 2)
- "Your family has confidence that you are safe. That's a very powerful message." — The expert sees family peace of mind as a major selling point for the older demographic.
- This is distinct from the user's own confidence — it's about the buyer's family feeling relieved.
- Should be a headline or prominent callout.

### Theme 7: The page must work WITHOUT the video (new from Review 2)
- Expert approach: scroll first, then optionally watch the video. If the page doesn't make sense before the video, it's failed.
- "I literally got none of that when I scroll through this page" — the video showed dual-grip stability, LED light, alarm. The page didn't communicate any of it prominently.
- Include product detail images/chips showing key features alongside the static content: "maybe we include some chips in here of them, of the rotating figureheads, of the rotating base, maybe of the alarm."

### Theme 8: Abstract icons < real product images (new from Review 2)
- Generic blue circle icons for features are "kind of odd." Real images of the actual product features would be better.
- "If you can get images in here, that would be better because I see what you're going for."

### Theme 9: Expert approaches pages fresh (new from Review 2)
- "I purposely try not to know much about this product." Expert doesn't research beforehand — mimics a real visitor.
- If the page can't explain the product to someone who knows nothing, it's failing its job.

## Language & Framing

The expert uses these specific framings that the agent should adopt:

- **"Blast them in the face"** — Value props need to be aggressive, unavoidable on scroll.
- **"This has to be boom, right there"** — Immediacy. The headline/value must hit instantly.
- **"This is not solving a job"** — Features that don't connect to a user need are dismissed this way.
- **"Where is that being highlighted?"** — When a great value prop exists but is buried or tiny.
- **"It's just not powerful enough"** — Used when something is present but doesn't have enough visual weight.
- **"This is wasted"** — Used when a strong section (like "Real Benefits You'll Feel") doesn't actually deliver the benefits prominently.
- **"Keep things as simple as possible"** — Simplicity is a recurring principle. Less is more.
- **"Value-oriented phrases"** — Headlines should be value-oriented, not clever or vague.
- **"Relatively meaningless phrases"** — Used for vague aspirational copy like "Power your walk with smart support." If it could apply to any product, it communicates nothing. (Review 2)
- **"I got none of that"** — Used when the video/product is great but the page fails to convey it. (Review 2)
- **"This is completely wasted here"** — Used when strong selling points exist only in the video or in hard-to-find places, not prominently on the page. (Review 2)
- **"Why is this not up here?"** — Used when great copy (like "Step with Joy. Walk with Confidence") is buried far down the page instead of near the top. (Review 2)

### Good Enough:
- "Stay active from the comfort of your chair" — Expert likes this headline. It's clear, outcome-focused, and speaks to the audience. The problem is placement, not the copy itself.
- "Stay active without leaving your seat" — Expert called this "really powerful."
- Lifestyle images showing elderly users and wheelchair users — "I like this a lot" and "that speaks to me"
- Testimonials section with real people — "Fantastic testimonials. There's people involved."
- The page overall: "I do like it I think it's very pretty" — visual design is acceptable.
- "Step with Joy. Walk with Confidence" — Expert called this "fantastic" on the cane page. Good emotional impact copy. Problem is placement (buried), not quality. (Review 2)
- "Real support. Real stability. Real confidence in every step" — Expert noted as good but too small. Sizing/prominence issue, not copy issue. (Review 2)
- Testimonials with product-in-use photos — "I like these testimonials, this is really cool." (Review 2)
- "Stands on its own, no tipping" — "That's really good." Specific, differentiating feature. (Review 2)

### Needs Work (Dealbreakers):
- Headline not visible above the fold on mobile — **immediate fail, #1 priority**.
- Promotional banners for unrelated products above the fold — must be removed for landing page traffic.
- Multiple different CTAs with different copy and colors — must unify.
- CTAs that are too small on mobile — must be full-width.
- Value props buried in tiny text that can't be caught during fast scroll — must be made prominent.
- Feature specs (LCD display, anti-slip pedals) presented as selling points without benefit context — replace with outcome language.
- Hero image that doesn't match the headline promise — if you say "comfort of your chair," show someone in a chair.
- **Page that can't explain product without video** — if visitor scrolls full page and doesn't understand what the product does, that's a fundamental failure. (Review 2)
- **Vague "smart" claims without explanation** — "I don't know what smart means" — calling a product "smart" without immediately showing why is confusing, not impressive. (Review 2)
- **Best copy buried at the bottom** — "Step with Joy. Walk with Confidence" was near the footer instead of near the hero. Best copy must be placed high. (Review 2)
- **Abstract icons instead of real product images** — Generic blue icons for features are "kind of odd." Use actual product photos. (Review 2)
- **Large white space without value props** — PDP-style white space sections on landing pages are "distracting" and "wasted" if they don't contain benefit messaging. (Review 2)

## Industry-Specific Preferences

### E-commerce / Home Fitness (Older Demographic):
- Zero fall risk is a massive, underutilized value prop for this audience.
- "Low impact" should be front and center — this audience fears injury.
- Remote control is a key differentiator — highlight the convenience for limited mobility users.
- Text must be large enough for 60-90 year olds to read on mobile.
- Compact and portable matters for long workday use cases — but needs to be highlighted more prominently, not buried in a small card.
- Reducing swelling and boosting circulation are the health outcomes this audience cares about most.
- Consider reducing the number of benefit cards and making fewer, bigger, more impactful ones.
- Sticky buy box on mobile is noted as existing on the real site (good).

### E-commerce / Mobility Aids (Older Demographic) — added from Review 2:
- **Family peace of mind is a headline-worthy value prop**: "Your family has confidence that you are safe. That's a very powerful message." Frame the product as giving peace of mind to BOTH the user and their family.
- **"Reduce your fall risk"** and **"stay more stable while walking"** are the top headline directions the expert suggests for canes/walking aids.
- **"Feel confident in the dark"** — LED light safety is a strong emotional hook for this audience.
- **Foldable/compact must be prominently stated** — Expert couldn't find "compact" on the page even though the cane folds. "I'm not seeing compact anywhere because it folds up and pops out."
- **"Stands on its own, no tipping"** — Self-standing is a meaningful differentiator worth calling out.
- **Show product feature images, not abstract icons** — For mobility aids, show the actual rotating base, the actual alarm button, the actual LED light. Not generic blue circles.
- **The product video can be great, but the page must work without it** — Static content must communicate everything the video shows. Include product detail chips/images showing key features alongside text.
- **Expert-suggested headline formula for this category**: [Benefit headline] + [Product image showing that benefit]. E.g., "Reduce your fall risk" + image of the 360 base.

## Expert Analysis Method

How the expert approaches a page (replicate this flow):
1. **Open on mobile** — always start with mobile view.
2. **Check above the fold** — is the headline visible? What's consuming the space?
3. **Fast scroll** — scroll the full page quickly like a real user from TikTok/Meta. What did you catch? What did you miss?
4. **Identify what's powerful** — call out sections that work ("Stay active without leaving your seat" = powerful).
5. **Identify what's wasted** — sections with great potential but poor execution (hidden value props, tiny text).
6. **Check CTA consistency** — are all CTAs the same copy, color, and size?
7. **Check image/copy alignment** — do images support the headline's promise?
8. **Simplify** — what can be removed or consolidated?
