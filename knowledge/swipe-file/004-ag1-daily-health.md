# Swipe Card: AG1 — "What Is AG1?" Landing Page

**URL**: https://drinkag1.com/learn/ag1
**Date Analyzed**: 2026-03-10
**Vertical**: Health & Wellness / Supplements (DTC daily health drink subscription)
**Target Audience**: Health-conscious adults 25-50, fitness-oriented, willing to invest in premium daily nutrition, looking to simplify their supplement stack
**Overall Impression**: A cinematic, premium-feeling education page that builds conviction through layered proof — social proof above the headline, a comparison table that visually eliminates alternatives, and clinical data with specific numbers. The page is long and thorough, designed for someone who needs to be convinced that one product can replace their entire supplement shelf. It's a brand page, not a pure landing page — full nav is present — but the conversion architecture is razor-sharp.

4. Headline: "The #1 Daily Health Drink"
5. Subheadline: "One scoop of AG1 Next Gen delivers 75+ ingredients, covering your multivitamin, pre- and probiotics, superfoods and greens, and more in one clinically-studied Daily Health Drink.*"

7. Trust strip: "Backed by 4 clinical trials¹⁻⁴" | "NSF Certified for Sport®" | "Trusted by leading scientists and athletes" (icons + text)

**What's deliberately present**: Social proof ABOVE the headline — the review count comes first, establishing credibility before the claim. This is the opposite of most pages that put the headline first.

**What's deliberately absent**: No price above the fold. No product image in the hero (just lifestyle). No discount or offer language. This is a brand authority play, not a deal-driven page.

## Headline Formula

**Headline**: "The #1 Daily Health Drink"
**Angle**: Social proof / category leadership claim — "#1" is a dominance assertion
**Word count**: 5 words
**Subheadline**: "One scoop of AG1 Next Gen delivers 75+ ingredients, covering your multivitamin, pre- and probiotics, superfoods and greens, and more in one clinically-studied Daily Health Drink.*"
**Analysis**: The headline is a category claim, not a benefit statement. "The #1 Daily Health Drink" establishes dominance — it assumes the category leader position rather than arguing for it. This works because the proof (50K reviews) appears ABOVE the headline, pre-validating the claim. The subheadline does the heavy lifting: it names the product generation ("Next Gen"), quantifies the value (75+ ingredients), lists what it replaces (multivitamin, probiotics, superfoods, greens), and establishes clinical backing — all in one sentence. The asterisk after "Daily Health Drink" is smart legal compliance that doesn't break the flow.

- Cross-sell section: "Shop Now →" + "Learn About AGZ"

**CTA consistency**: Multiple CTA pairs throughout — always a primary action ("Shop AG1" / "Shop Now") paired with an education path ("See Our Studies" / "How Benefits Compound" / "View All Ingredients"). This dual-path strategy respects different stages of buyer readiness.

**What's notable**: The persistent dual CTA architecture — every conversion point offers BOTH a buy path and a learn path. This is ideal for premium products where some visitors need more information before buying. AG1 never forces a premature purchase decision.

## Social Proof Strategy

**Types used**:
- **Review count (primary, above headline)**: "50,000+ verified 5-star reviews for AG1 products" — with green stars. Appears ABOVE the headline and again before the review section
- **Clinical trials**: "Backed by 4 clinical trials¹⁻⁴" — in trust strip, with superscript citations
- **Third-party certification**: "NSF Certified for Sport®" — official badge + name
- **Expert authority**: "Trusted by leading scientists and athletes" — in trust strip
- **Clinical data carousel**: "CLINICALLY SHOWN TO ENRICH THE GUT MICROBIOME¹⁻³" — "10x increase in healthy bacteria in the gut" — specific quantified clinical result
- **Customer reviews**: 3 named review cards with dates (Louis C., Ashley C., Wendy O.) — each with star rating, title, quote, name, date, and source type
- **Third-party testing**: "Third party tested for purity and potency"

**What's notable**: AG1 uses CLINICAL proof as its primary social proof — not customer count or influencer endorsement. The "4 clinical trials" and "10x increase in healthy bacteria" are specific, scientific claims that position AG1 above typical supplement marketing. The review section appears near the bottom (after clinical proof), subordinating crowd validation to scientific validation. This is a deliberate hierarchy: science first, customers second.

## Visual Design

**Colors**:
- Background: Cream/warm off-white (#F5F0E8 approx) for most sections — NOT pure white
- Primary: Dark teal/forest green (#1A3C34 approx) — used for hero overlay, ingredient section, CTA buttons
- Accent: Bright green (#1DB954 approx) — CTAs, checkmarks in comparison table, stars
- Text: Near-black on light backgrounds, white on dark backgrounds
- Trust strip: On dark hero background, white text
- Overall: Premium, editorial, almost magazine-like. The warm off-white background is distinctive — it avoids the clinical feel of pure white while maintaining cleanliness

**Fonts**:
- Headlines: Modern serif (editorial, elegant) — lowercase/mixed case, generous sizing
- Body: Clean sans-serif
- Labels/categories: Monospaced uppercase (e.g., "NUTRITION & SLEEP", "FARM TO LABEL") — creates editorial magazine feel
- CTA text: Mixed case, sans-serif

**Standout visual technique**: The comparison table uses AG1's brand green as a column background with bright green checkmarks vs. gray X circles for competitors — the visual weight is overwhelmingly in AG1's favor. You don't even need to read the table to understand the message.

**Photography**: Cinematic lifestyle-first approach — every photo is aspirational (outdoor athletics, hiking, kitchen scenes). The product is shown being USED in daily life, not in studio isolation. The ingredient photography (bilberry, magnesium, apple) is art-directed like food magazine content.

## Mobile Execution

The page uses a clean desktop layout that would stack naturally on mobile. The full-bleed hero image with centered text works well at any viewport. The comparison table would need horizontal scroll on mobile (potentially problematic). The 4-across benefit cards would stack to 1-2 per row. The dual CTA pairs would stack vertically. No sticky CTA was observed — the page relies on multiple CTA insertion points throughout the scroll. The extensive FAQ section provides good mobile scroll-through content.

## The Standout Move

**Social proof above the headline — validating the claim before making it.** Most pages follow: headline → proof. AG1 flips it: proof → headline. By placing "★★★★★ 50,000+ verified 5-star reviews" above "The #1 Daily Health Drink," they've already earned the right to make a bold claim before the visitor reads it. This technique works because:
1. The category claim ("#1") would feel arrogant without proof — but the proof precedes it
2. The visitor's first impression is social validation, not a marketing claim
3. It creates a psychological sequence: "lots of people love this" → "oh, it's the #1 health drink" → "let me learn more"
4. Combined with the dual-CTA education path ("See Our Studies"), it signals "we have nothing to hide"

This is especially powerful for premium health products where skepticism is high and the customer needs to believe the science before they'll spend $79/month.

## Steal List

- **Social proof above the headline** — Place your best trust signal (review count, user count, clinical data) ABOVE the headline, not below it. Pre-validate the claim before making it. This is a power move for any product making a bold positioning statement.
- **Comparison table as category killer** — "AG1 is a more-in-one solution" with a visual table showing AG1 = all checkmarks vs. individual products = partial coverage. For any product that replaces multiple alternatives, this format is devastating.
- **Dual CTA at every conversion point** — Always pair a buy CTA with an education CTA ("Shop AG1" + "See Our Studies"). This respects buyer readiness and actually increases total conversions by serving both ready-to-buy and need-more-info visitors.
- **Clinical proof with specific numbers** — "10x increase in healthy bacteria in the gut" is infinitely more convincing than "clinically proven." Always show the actual clinical result, not just the claim of having studies.
- **Per-day price framing for subscriptions** — "Less than $3/day" makes $79/month feel like a rounding error. For any subscription product, always show the daily cost alongside the monthly total.
- **Warm off-white background** — Using cream/off-white instead of pure white creates a premium, editorial feel that distinguishes the brand from clinical supplement pages. Small design choice, big perception shift.
- **Monospaced uppercase category labels** — "FARM TO LABEL", "QUALITY & TESTING", "CLINICAL RESEARCH" — creates an editorial/magazine taxonomy that elevates perceived quality. Makes marketing content feel like journalism.
- **Late price reveal** — Price doesn't appear until section 11 of 14. By the time you see $79/mo, you've consumed comparison data, clinical proof, ingredient transparency, and benefit descriptions. The price feels earned and reasonable.
