# Swipe Card: Qure — Micro-Infusion Facial System

**URL**: https://qureskincare.com/pages/qure-microinfusion-offer
**Date Analyzed**: 2026-03-10
**Vertical**: Beauty / Skincare (premium at-home treatment device)
**Target Audience**: Women 35-60, anti-aging focused, willing to invest, clinic-treatment seekers
**Overall Impression**: Authority-first page that leads with dermatologist endorsements before product features. Heavy use of before/after photos as primary proof mechanism. Tiered bundle pricing with aggressive anchoring ($719→$219) and scarcity ("164 kits left"). The sticky bottom bar is relentless and effective.

2. Pre-headline: Expert avatar row (3-4 doctor headshots) + "Backed by Skin Experts"
3. Headline: "Visibly reduce the appearance of wrinkles and dark spots - without injectables."
4. Subheadline: "24K gold microneedles deliver targeted serums beyond your skin's barrier where traditional creams can't reach."
5. CTA: "START YOUR TREATMENT" (black, full-width on its column)
6. Below CTA: "Limited Time Offer: Save Up to 70%"
7. Hero image: Close-up of woman using the micro-infusion device on her face — product in use, showing application

**What's deliberately absent**: No star ratings above the fold. No review count. The authority signal is DOCTORS, not customers. This is a considered purchase — clinical credibility matters more than crowd validation at first impression.

## Headline Formula

**Headline**: "Visibly reduce the appearance of wrinkles and dark spots - without injectables."
**Angle**: Direct outcome + alternative positioning (replaces injectables/Botox)
**Word count**: 13 words
**Subheadline**: "24K gold microneedles deliver targeted serums beyond your skin's barrier where traditional creams can't reach."
**Analysis**: This is a differentiation headline — it tells you the outcome (reduce wrinkles + dark spots), names what it replaces (injectables), and immediately implies the category (medical-grade but at-home). The subheadline explains the mechanism with premium material specificity ("24K gold"). "Where traditional creams can't reach" positions every other product as inferior.

## CTA Strategy

**CTAs found**:
- Hero: "START YOUR TREATMENT" — black, large, uppercase
- Sticky bottom bar: "BUY NOW & SAVE UP TO 70%" — dark, persistent across entire page
- Mid-page: "BUY NOW & SAVE UP TO 70%" after the 5 reasons section
- In purchase configurator: Add to cart within the tiered pricing module

**CTA consistency**: Two variations — "START YOUR TREATMENT" (action + identity) and "BUY NOW & SAVE UP TO 70%" (value-lead)
**Sticky bar**: Bottom-anchored, shows "Micro-Infusion Facial System" + CTA + "1,293 purchased in last 48 hours" (social proof urgency)
**What's notable**: The hero CTA says "START YOUR TREATMENT" — not "Buy" or "Shop." This language frames the product as a medical treatment you're beginning, not a product you're buying. This elevates perceived value and justifies premium pricing.

## Social Proof Strategy

**Types used**:
- **Expert authority (primary)**: 4+ Board-Certified Dermatologists with photos, names, credentials, and quotes — this is the FIRST thing after the hero
- **Before/after photos**: Tabbed by skin concern (Fine Lines, Tone, Glow) with customer name, verification badge, and treatment count ("After 3 treatments")
- **Customer B/A gallery**: "Real stories, real results" — separate section with 3+ verified customers
- **Purchase velocity**: "1,293 purchased in last 48 hours" in sticky bar — real-time social proof
- **Scarcity**: "164 kits left" / "201 kits left" next to each bundle option

**What's notable**: Before/after photos are the hero of social proof, not written testimonials. In skincare, seeing is believing. The photos are organized by CONCERN (not random) so visitors can find proof relevant to their specific issue. This is personalized social proof — much more persuasive than generic reviews.

## Mobile Execution

The sticky bottom bar would be prominent on mobile — always showing the CTA and purchase velocity stat. The before/after photos are compelling at any viewport. The tiered pricing cards would stack well. The doctor carousel would swipe naturally on touch.

## The Standout Move

**Authority-first page architecture.** Most landing pages follow: headline → benefits → proof → CTA. Qure flips it: headline → DOCTOR AUTHORITY → mechanism → proof → CTA. By leading with 4 board-certified dermatologists before even explaining the product, they answer the biggest objection for premium skincare: "Does this actually work?" The answer comes from doctors, not the brand. This is especially powerful for:
- Products that seem too good to be true
- Premium price points that need justification
- Categories with lots of skepticism (beauty devices, supplements)

## Steal List

- **Expert avatars above the headline** — Doctor/expert headshot row + "Backed by Skin Experts" as the FIRST element visitors see. Instant credibility before a word of copy is read.
- **Before/after organized by concern** — Tab interface letting visitors choose their concern, then showing relevant B/A photos. Personalized proof > random proof.
- **"START YOUR TREATMENT" CTA language** — Frames purchase as beginning a medical treatment. Elevates perceived value. Useful for any product that can be positioned as a "system" or "program."
- **Purchase velocity in sticky bar** — "1,293 purchased in last 48 hours" is more compelling than a static review count because it implies momentum and current demand.
- **Per-treatment price framing** — "$18.25 per treatment" vs "$219" — always show the smallest defensible unit cost alongside the total.
- **Scarcity per SKU** — "164 kits left" on the best-value option vs "201 kits left" on the smaller bundle. Different scarcity levels per tier push toward the higher-value option (it's selling faster = more popular = better deal).
- **Treatment count on B/A photos** — "After 3 treatments" labels set expectations AND sell larger bundles simultaneously.
