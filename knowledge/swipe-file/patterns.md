### Copy Styles

### CTA Frequency
- Average CTAs per page: ~3-4 (range: 1–7+)
- Most common placement points: Hero section, mid-page after benefits/proof, bottom near price
- **Insight**: There's no single "right" answer — Ridge uses 1 CTA at the bottom (education-first), AG1 uses 7+ throughout, Jolie uses ~2 (Add to Cart + subscription variant). What matters is alignment with the page's purpose.

### CTA Consistency
- Pages with identical CTAs throughout: 2 (Ridge — single "SHOP NOW"; Jolie — single "Add to Cart" with subscription variant)
- Pages with varied CTAs: 3 (Grüns, Qure, AG1 — all use 2+ CTA variations)
- **Insight**: Most great pages vary their CTA copy by context. The exceptions (Ridge, Jolie) work because both are single-decision-point pages — one product, one action.

### Sticky/Floating CTA
- Pages using sticky CTA: 2 (Grüns: sticky header with social proof + CTA; Qure: sticky bottom bar with product name + CTA + purchase velocity)
- Pages without: 3 (Ridge: no sticky — single CTA at bottom; AG1: no sticky — relies on multiple CTA insertion points; Jolie: no sticky — minimalist PDP, short page)
- **Insight**: Sticky CTAs appear on pages with strong offers/discounts. Pure education, brand, or short PDP pages skip them.

## Narrative Arc Patterns

### Most Common Section Order
Based on 5 pages, the emerging pattern is:
1. **Authority/trust signal** (above or immediately below headline)
2. **Differentiation** (comparison, mechanism, or education)
3. **Benefits/outcomes** (what you'll get)
4. **Social proof** (reviews, testimonials, B/A)
5. **Price/offer** (conversion point)
6. **Objection handling** (FAQ, guarantees)

### Story Structures
| Structure | Count |
|-----------|-------|
| Problem → Solution → Proof → CTA | 0 |
| Hero → Benefits → Proof → Offer | 1 (Grüns) |
| Pain → Agitate → Solution → Close | 0 |
| Authority-First → Education → Proof → Convert | 2 (Qure, AG1) |
| Education-First (Listicle) → Single CTA | 1 (Ridge) |
| Minimalist PDP → Product Image → Social Proof → Convert | 1 (Jolie) |

**Insight**: The most successful pattern is Authority/Education FIRST, then benefits, then conversion. 3 of 5 pages lead with credibility before selling. Grüns leads with its offer (52% off). Jolie is a different archetype entirely — a minimalist PDP where the product image and review volume do the convincing. Short-form PDP pages can skip the education arc when brand aesthetic + social proof volume are strong enough.

### Non-Negotiables (80%+ of great pages do this)

1. **`[swipe-file]` Light background, bold accents** — All 5 pages use white or off-white backgrounds with strong accent colors. Premium = clean + confident. (100%)
2. **`[swipe-file]` Use at least 2 types of social proof** — No page relies on a single proof mechanism. Layer expert authority + customer proof, or reviews + risk reversal, etc. (100%)
3. **`[swipe-file]` Hero image must show product in use, lifestyle, or premium studio shot** — Every page uses a product-contextual hero. 4/5 show product-in-use/lifestyle; Jolie uses a premium studio shot that functions as brand aesthetic persuasion. (100%)
4. **`[swipe-file]` Use price anchoring** — 4/5 pages show a crossed-out higher price, subscription discount, or explicit comparison to alternatives. (80%)
5. **`[swipe-file]` Establish authority or volume trust early** — 4/5 pages establish credibility through experts, clinical data, certifications, or influencer partnerships. Jolie substitutes with high-volume reviews (2,242). Either way: credibility appears EARLY. (80%)
6. **`[swipe-file]` Include social proof above the fold** — 4/5 pages put a trust signal (stars, reviews, expert badges) in the first viewport. (80%)

1. **`[swipe-file]` Use per-unit/per-day price framing** — 3/5 pages break the price into the smallest defensible unit ($1.46/day, $18.25/treatment, <$3/day). Always show this alongside the total. (60%)
2. **`[swipe-file]` Vary CTA copy by context** — 3/5 pages use different CTA text at different points in the page, tailored to the section's role. (60%)

### Secret Weapons (used by 1-2 pages but high impact)

1. **Social proof above the headline** (AG1) — Place the review count or trust signal BEFORE the headline. Pre-validates the claim before the visitor even reads it. Especially powerful for bold positioning statements.
2. **Authority-first architecture** (Qure) — Lead with expert credentials before explaining the product. Answer "does this work?" with doctors FIRST, then explain what it is. Essential for premium products with high skepticism.
3. **Sticky header = social proof + CTA** (Grüns) — Put your best trust numbers AND your primary CTA in one persistent top bar. Every scroll position has both trust and conversion available. Most efficient use of sticky real estate.
4. **Customer quote as section header** (Ridge) — "I WISH I'D TRIED IT SOONER" used as page architecture, not buried in a testimonial card. Makes social proof unmissable.
5. **Comparison table as category killer** (AG1) — Visual table showing your product = all checkmarks vs. alternatives = partial coverage. For any product that replaces multiple alternatives, this format is devastating.
6. **Dual CTA (buy + educate)** (AG1) — At every conversion point, pair "Shop" with "Learn More" / "See Studies." Respects different buyer readiness levels and increases total conversions.
7. **Purchase velocity in sticky bar** (Qure) — "1,293 purchased in last 48 hours" implies momentum and current demand. More compelling than static review counts.
8. **Before/after organized by concern** (Qure) — Tab interface letting visitors choose their concern, then showing relevant B/A photos. Personalized proof > random proof.
9. **No navigation on LP** (Grüns) — Zero links to homepage, categories, or anything else. Only path: scroll, click CTA, or leave. Radical focus maximizes conversion.
10. **Numbered listicle as page architecture** (Ridge) — Commit to a single structural concept ("6 reasons") and execute cleanly. Creates scroll contract, momentum, and completion desire.
11. **"START YOUR TREATMENT" CTA framing** (Qure) — Frames purchase as beginning a medical treatment, not buying a product. Elevates perceived value. Works for any product positioned as a "system" or "program."
12. **Over-deliver on content promise** (Grüns) — Headline says "6 Reasons" but delivers 7+. Exceeding expectations builds trust subconsciously.
13. **Scarcity per SKU** (Qure) — "164 kits left" on the premium bundle vs "201 kits left" on the smaller one. Different scarcity levels per tier push toward the higher-value option.
14. **Late price reveal** (AG1) — Price doesn't appear until section 11 of 14. Maximum value building before the ask.
15. **Category reframing through visual design** (Jolie) — Design a commodity product's page like the aspirational adjacent category. Jolie designs a showerhead page like Glossier/Aesop, not Moen/Delta. Changes the competitive set, reference price, and buyer mindset. Works for: kitchen appliances styled as chef's tools, supplements as luxury beauty, office furniture as design objects.
16. **Announcement bar as risk reversal** (Jolie) — "Free shipping & free 60 day returns" is the first thing visitors see. Pre-emptively neutralizes the two biggest e-commerce objections before the product image loads.
17. **Color variants as engagement hooks** (Jolie) — 5 curated color options with fashion-brand naming ("Brushed Gold," "Jet Black") force visitors into choice-making mindset (ownership psychology) and signal premium craftsmanship.
18. **Subscription price anchoring** (Jolie) — $98 with subscription vs $169 standalone. Visitor focuses on "saving $71" rather than "committing to $140/year in filters." Razor-and-blade model presented as customer benefit.
19. **Minimalist copy as premium signal** (Jolie) — Entire product description is one sentence. Restraint signals confidence — "we don't need to convince you with words." Only works with strong visual design + strong social proof to compensate.
20. **Content-rich navigation as brand depth signal** (Jolie) — Nav includes lifestyle content sections (Shower Thoughts, The Drip blog). Signals a lifestyle brand with depth, not a single-product Shopify store. Increases trust even if visitors never click.
21. **One product, one price, one decision** (Jolie) — Radical simplicity eliminates decision paralysis. The only choice is color. For single-SKU DTC brands, simplicity converts — no "good/better/best" tier needed.

## Contradictions & Surprises

Pages that break emerging patterns in interesting ways:

1. **Ridge has NO CTA above the fold** — While 3/4 pages include a CTA in the first viewport, Ridge deliberately omits it. The page is designed as a scroll-through education experience with a single CTA at the bottom. This works for top/mid-funnel education pages, NOT for direct response.

2. **Ridge has only ONE CTA on the entire page** — Most pages have 3-7+ CTAs. Ridge proves that for education-first content, a single well-earned CTA can be more effective than many.

3. **AG1 has NO sticky CTA despite being the longest page** — On a 14-section page, AG1 relies on multiple CTA insertion points rather than a persistent bar. This suggests the "brand page" archetype prefers embedded CTAs over sticky ones.

4. **Grüns has NO navigation** — While 3/4 pages retain navigation, Grüns removes everything. This radical focus shows confidence — the page IS the funnel, no escape hatches needed.

5. **Qure leads with doctors BEFORE product explanation** — Most pages explain the product first, then prove it works. Qure proves credibility first (doctors), then explains the mechanism. This inversion is especially powerful for "too good to be true" products.

6. **AG1's headline is only 5 words** — The shortest by far (average is 10). A category claim ("#1 Daily Health Drink") can be powerful when pre-validated by social proof above it. Brevity works when proof does the heavy lifting.

7. **Two pages use numbered listicle structure** (Ridge, Grüns) — This is the closest thing to a "template" we've found. The listicle format works across verticals (EDC and supplements) because it creates a natural content promise and scroll contract.

8. **Jolie shows price above the fold** — While 4/5 pages defer pricing, Jolie puts $169 right in the hero. This works because: (a) it's a PDP format where price is expected, (b) the subscription option ($98) reframes $169 as "expensive" by comparison, making the subscription the obvious choice, and (c) 2,242 reviews pre-validate the price. **Takeaway**: price above the fold works on short PDPs with subscription anchoring, not on long-form education pages.

9. **Jolie has NO authority signals** — While 4/5 pages establish expert/clinical/influencer credibility, Jolie uses zero. No dermatologists, no clinical trials, no influencer endorsements. Volume social proof (2,242 reviews at 4.8 stars) carries the entire trust burden. This suggests that for consumer products in the $100-200 range, crowd validation alone can suffice — but for anything with health claims or premium pricing above $200, expert authority is likely still essential.

10. **Jolie uses almost no marketing copy** — While every other page invests heavily in benefit-driven copy, headlines, and narrative arcs, Jolie's product description is a single sentence. The visual design does the selling. This is the fashion-brand approach to DTC — it requires a genuinely beautiful product, professional photography, and strong brand aesthetics to pull off.
