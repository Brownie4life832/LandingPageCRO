## CTA Patterns

### Copy Styles
| Style | Count | Examples |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Value-driven ("Save X%") | 2 | Grüns: "Save 52% + Free Shipping"; Qure: "BUY NOW & SAVE UP TO 70%" |
| Action-only ("Buy/Shop Now") | 2 | Ridge: "SHOP NOW"; AG1: "Shop AG1 →", "Shop Now" |

| Low-commitment / education | 1 | AG1: "See Our Studies", "How Benefits Compound", "View All Ingredients" |

### CTA Frequency
- Average CTAs per page: ~4 (range: 1–7+)
- Most common placement points: Hero section, mid-page after benefits/proof, bottom near price
- **Insight**: There's no single "right" answer — Ridge uses 1 CTA at the bottom (education-first), AG1 uses 7+ throughout. What matters is alignment with the page's purpose.

### CTA Consistency
- Pages with identical CTAs throughout: 1 (Ridge — single "SHOP NOW")
- Pages with varied CTAs: 3 (Grüns, Qure, AG1 — all use 2+ CTA variations)
- **Insight**: Most great pages vary their CTA copy by context. The exception (Ridge) works because the page is a pure education funnel with one decision point.

### Sticky/Floating CTA
- Pages using sticky CTA: 2 (Grüns: sticky header with social proof + CTA; Qure: sticky bottom bar with product name + CTA + purchase velocity)
- Pages without: 2 (Ridge: no sticky — single CTA at bottom; AG1: no sticky — relies on multiple CTA insertion points)
- **Insight**: Sticky CTAs appear on pages with strong offers/discounts. Pure education or brand pages skip them.

## Narrative Arc Patterns

### Most Common Section Order
Based on 4 pages, the emerging pattern is:
1. **Authority/trust signal** (above or immediately below headline)
2. **Differentiation** (comparison, mechanism, or education)
3. **Benefits/outcomes** (what you'll get)
4. **Social proof** (reviews, testimonials, B/A)
5. **Price/offer** (conversion point)
6. **Objection handling** (FAQ, guarantees)

### Story Structures
| Structure | Count |
|-----------|-------|
| Problem → Solution → Proof → CTA | 0 |
| Hero → Benefits → Proof → Offer | 1 (Grüns) |
| Pain → Agitate → Solution → Close | 0 |
| Authority-First → Education → Proof → Convert | 2 (Qure, AG1) |
| Education-First (Listicle) → Single CTA | 1 (Ridge) |

**Insight**: The most successful pattern is Authority/Education FIRST, then benefits, then conversion. 3 of 4 pages lead with credibility before selling. Only Grüns leads with its offer (52% off), and even Grüns puts social proof in the sticky header from the start.

## Rules From the Swipe File

Synthesized rules that emerge from the data. Tagged with `[swipe-file]` to distinguish from expert calibration rules.

### Non-Negotiables (80%+ of great pages do this)
*Note: With only 4 pages, these are directional. Will solidify after 5+ pages.*

1. **`[swipe-file]` NEVER show price above the fold** — 4/4 pages omit pricing from the first viewport. Build value before revealing cost. (100%)
2. **`[swipe-file]` Always include authority signals** — Every page establishes credibility through experts, clinical data, certifications, or influencer partnerships. Authority appears EARLY, not buried below the fold. (100%)
3. **`[swipe-file]` Light background, bold accents** — All 4 pages use white or off-white backgrounds with strong accent colors. Premium = clean + confident. (100%)
4. **`[swipe-file]` Hero image must show product in use or lifestyle** — No studio product-only shots above the fold. Show the product in context (Ridge: wallet in hand, Qure: device on face, AG1: athlete lifestyle, Grüns: woman eating gummy). (100%)
5. **`[swipe-file]` Use at least 2 types of social proof** — No page relies on a single proof mechanism. Layer expert authority + customer proof, or reviews + clinical data, etc. (100%)

### Strong Patterns (60-79% frequency)
*Directional at 4 pages — tracking toward potential non-negotiables:*

1. **`[swipe-file]` Use per-unit/per-day price framing** — 3/4 pages break the price into the smallest defensible unit ($1.46/day, $18.25/treatment, <$3/day). Always show this alongside the total. (75%)
2. **`[swipe-file]` Use price anchoring** — 3/4 pages show a crossed-out higher price next to the sale/subscription price. (75%)
3. **`[swipe-file]` Include social proof above the fold** — 3/4 pages put a trust signal (stars, reviews, expert badges) in the first viewport. (75%)
4. **`[swipe-file]` Vary CTA copy by context** — 3/4 pages use different CTA text at different points in the page, tailored to the section's role. (75%)
5. **`[swipe-file]` Serif headline + sans-serif body** — 3/4 pages use this typography combo for a premium editorial feel. (75%)

### Secret Weapons (used by 1-2 pages but high impact)

1. **Social proof above the headline** (AG1) — Place the review count or trust signal BEFORE the headline. Pre-validates the claim before the visitor even reads it. Especially powerful for bold positioning statements.
2. **Authority-first architecture** (Qure) — Lead with expert credentials before explaining the product. Answer "does this work?" with doctors FIRST, then explain what it is. Essential for premium products with high skepticism.
3. **Sticky header = social proof + CTA** (Grüns) — Put your best trust numbers AND your primary CTA in one persistent top bar. Every scroll position has both trust and conversion available. Most efficient use of sticky real estate.
4. **Customer quote as section header** (Ridge) — "I WISH I'D TRIED IT SOONER" used as page architecture, not buried in a testimonial card. Makes social proof unmissable.
5. **Comparison table as category killer** (AG1) — Visual table showing your product = all checkmarks vs. alternatives = partial coverage. For any product that replaces multiple alternatives, this format is devastating.
6. **Dual CTA (buy + educate)** (AG1) — At every conversion point, pair "Shop" with "Learn More" / "See Studies." Respects different buyer readiness levels and increases total conversions.
7. **Purchase velocity in sticky bar** (Qure) — "1,293 purchased in last 48 hours" implies momentum and current demand. More compelling than static review counts.
8. **Before/after organized by concern** (Qure) — Tab interface letting visitors choose their concern, then showing relevant B/A photos. Personalized proof > random proof.
9. **No navigation on LP** (Grüns) — Zero links to homepage, categories, or anything else. Only path: scroll, click CTA, or leave. Radical focus maximizes conversion.
10. **Numbered listicle as page architecture** (Ridge) — Commit to a single structural concept ("6 reasons") and execute cleanly. Creates scroll contract, momentum, and completion desire.
11. **"START YOUR TREATMENT" CTA framing** (Qure) — Frames purchase as beginning a medical treatment, not buying a product. Elevates perceived value. Works for any product positioned as a "system" or "program."
12. **Over-deliver on content promise** (Grüns) — Headline says "6 Reasons" but delivers 7+. Exceeding expectations builds trust subconsciously.
13. **Scarcity per SKU** (Qure) — "164 kits left" on the premium bundle vs "201 kits left" on the smaller one. Different scarcity levels per tier push toward the higher-value option.
14. **Late price reveal** (AG1) — Price doesn't appear until section 11 of 14. Maximum value building before the ask.

## Contradictions & Surprises

Pages that break emerging patterns in interesting ways:

1. **Ridge has NO CTA above the fold** — While 3/4 pages include a CTA in the first viewport, Ridge deliberately omits it. The page is designed as a scroll-through education experience with a single CTA at the bottom. This works for top/mid-funnel education pages, NOT for direct response.

2. **Ridge has only ONE CTA on the entire page** — Most pages have 3-7+ CTAs. Ridge proves that for education-first content, a single well-earned CTA can be more effective than many.

3. **AG1 has NO sticky CTA despite being the longest page** — On a 14-section page, AG1 relies on multiple CTA insertion points rather than a persistent bar. This suggests the "brand page" archetype prefers embedded CTAs over sticky ones.

4. **Grüns has NO navigation** — While 3/4 pages retain navigation, Grüns removes everything. This radical focus shows confidence — the page IS the funnel, no escape hatches needed.

5. **Qure leads with doctors BEFORE product explanation** — Most pages explain the product first, then prove it works. Qure proves credibility first (doctors), then explains the mechanism. This inversion is especially powerful for "too good to be true" products.

6. **AG1's headline is only 5 words** — The shortest by far (average is 10). A category claim ("#1 Daily Health Drink") can be powerful when pre-validated by social proof above it. Brevity works when proof does the heavy lifting.

7. **Two pages use numbered listicle structure** (Ridge, Grüns) — This is the closest thing to a "template" we've found. The listicle format works across verticals (EDC and supplements) because it creates a natural content promise and scroll contract.
